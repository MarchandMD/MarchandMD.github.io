link to original: <a href="https://www.ivanturkovic.com/2026/02/25/ai-made-writing-code-easier-engineering-harder/" target="_blank">AI Made Writing Code Easier. It Made Being an Engineer Harder.</a>

> The expected output of a software engineer in 2026 is dramatically higher than it was in 2023. Not because anyone held a meeting and announced new targets. Not because your manager sat you down and explained the new rules. The baseline just moved.

This is totally fair.

There is an implication that more _can_ be done, so more _will_ be done. Though there hasn't been any explicit directive, per se.

At least not in a professional capacity. Just a general "Adopt this" mentality.

Though that has been uttered, explicitly. "You (all) should be using these tools".

Considering all these tools are so new, and so plentiful, the efficacy or salience of any given tool is completely ambiguous. And forget feelings of objective craftsmanship or acumen...

Oh and BTW: LoC is not a relevant metric...

> A February 2026 study published in Harvard Business Review ...found... that ...Workers did not use AI to finish earlier and go home. They used it to do more.

I'm literally in the throws of re-vamping my personal blog, playing with vibe-coding for a side project, running copilot CLI, and claude code to do stuff.

Though Steve Yegge mentioned this as value creation without value capture in the AI vampire article....

> And for many engineers, no one acknowledged that the job they signed up for had fundamentally changed.

While I started prior to AI becoming prevalent, it wasn't much sooner.

So this new normal isn't exactly drastically different. I've always felt like I was treading water RE the code I wrote and the reviews I performed.

Gradually, over the course of time, confidence is built. And the hard edges of the craft become rounded down, to be a clearer picture of reality: No one is perfect.

So yes, the job has changed, but now it feels like everyone has experienced something that makes them question their approach to their own dev cycle.

> ... most software engineers became engineers because they love writing code.

Hmm, is it the code, or is it the problem-solving? And the contribution to something external?

Yes I love Ruby. And the community. And the syntactic sugar it and Rails affords.

I became an engineer because I wanted to unequivocally prove to myself that I could do hard things. Code is just the vehicle towards objective meritocracy, in my own eyes.

> ...not reviewing code

Reviewing code (of more skilled engineers) is a tremendous skill. Most engineers spend more time reading than writing.

In fact, reviewing code of a _bad_ engineer taught me a lot about software!

> The act of thinking through a problem, designing a solution, and expressing it precisely in a language that makes a machine do exactly what you intended. That is what drew most of us to this profession. It is a creative act, a form of craftsmanship, and for many engineers, the most satisfying part of their day.

Ah, ok. They're associating "write code" to "problem solve". Fair.

> Now they are being told to stop.

I mean, no one is stopping you (or any one) from hand writing code for yourself.... in a personal project. (I get this would mean 'do more', but hopefully there is a clear delineation between work and not-work. )

> .... The sense of craftsmanship went down.

I read another article titled the "Relocation of Rigor" which addresses this.

To paraphrase: the rigor of effort is simply relocated to another discipline.

I've elevated to a level of Musicianship in the past that most people will never achieve. At first I thought I was an artist simply because I held the guitar.

Until I learned that I barely knew how to hold my guitar.

Then my focus shifted to different things.

My rigor, relocated.

Similar idea.

> While engineers are being asked to write less code, they are simultaneously being asked to do more of everything else.

Earlier the author said no one explicitly said "do more". Now he's claiming they are being asked to write less code, and do more. Hmm...

> More product thinking. More architectural decision-making. More code review. More context switching. More planning. More testing oversight. More deployment awareness. More risk assessment.

Building from a TDD perspective bakes that part in.

I agree RE more risk assessment, context switching, planning.

But planning is also automated; and testing.

The automation has _allowed_ me to be more aware of risk surfaces, at a basic level. Which feels like growth.

> This is partly a direct consequence of AI acceleration. When code gets produced faster, the bottleneck shifts. It moves from implementation to everything surrounding implementation: requirements clarity, architecture decisions, integration testing, deployment strategy, monitoring, and maintenance. These were always part of the engineering lifecycle, but they were distributed across roles. Product managers handled requirements. QA handled testing. DevOps handled deployment. Senior architects handled system design.

Ok, bottlenecks, for sure. This keeps arising as a topic of conversation.

I wish requirements were clearer.

Let me make architecture decisions. And critique others.

There's still division of responsibility. Other parties now have less excuse for introducing friction.

Finally: accountability amongst the lazy.

> Now, with AI collapsing the implementation phase, organizations are quietly redistributing those responsibilities to the engineers themselves.

So pay me...

> The Harvard Business Review study documented this exact pattern. Product managers began writing code. Engineers took on product work. Researchers started doing engineering tasks. Roles that once had clear boundaries blurred as workers used AI to handle jobs that previously sat outside their remit.

I'll happily review code from PMs or Researchers. We can both grow. But again: pay me.

> ...a mid-level backend engineer is now expected to understand product strategy, review AI-generated frontend code they did not write, think about deployment infrastructure, consider security implications of code they cannot fully trace, and maintain a big-picture architectural awareness that used to be someone else’s job.

Agreed. This is too much.

So what is the implication? Are engineers becoming less relevant, or _more_ relevant?

> That is not empowerment. That is scope creep without a corresponding increase in compensation, authority, or time.

Preach brother....

> ...The engineers who survive are the ones who learn to say no, to prioritize ruthlessly, and to push back when the scope of their role quietly doubles without anyone acknowledging it.

Noted.

I've already begun listening for anything that sounds like an official declaration of "do more".

For now, I'm enjoying the opportunity to build my intrinsic value. Eventually, someone will pay through the nose...

> When AI writes code, you inherit the output without the reasoning....You are reviewing someone else’s work, except that someone is not a colleague you can ask questions. It is a statistical model that produces plausible-looking code without any understanding of your system’s specific constraints.

This is where planning comes into play.

Create a plan. Read it. Iterate. Challenge the model. Mold the plan. Make it a plan that makes total sense.

Not some sense. Not mostly makes sense.

Unequiviocally is a plan I understand from start to finish.

Then rip out the implementation, and build the tests.

Via prompt.

> A survey by Harness found that 67 percent of developers reported spending more time debugging AI-generated code, and 68 percent spent more time reviewing it than they did with human-written code.

AI tools aren't going away.

This sounds like engineers are actively fighting with the tools, rather than learning how to work with them.

> This is the supervision paradox. The faster AI generates code, the more human attention is required to ensure that code actually works in the context of a real system with real users and real business constraints. The production bottleneck did not disappear. It moved from writing to understanding, and understanding is harder to speed up.

But understanding _can_ speed up. Eventually.

Supervision, from one perspective, is a two way street: supervise both those below and above.

It's probably important for supervisors to play the role of intermediary between lower and higher. And work to set expectations in both directions.

> Workers did not consciously decide to work harder. The expansion happened naturally, almost invisibly.

Pomodoro technique seems more important than ever now. That and good old fashioned discipline.

> Now the only limit is your cognitive endurance. And most people do not know their cognitive limits until they have already blown past them.

I'm hearing Wolf of Wall Street lines here: "Gotta pump those numbers up..."

And people must learn self-regulation. Or not.

> Give your team real training. Not a lunch-and-learn about prompt engineering. Real investment in the skills that the new engineering landscape actually requires: system design, architectural thinking, product reasoning, security awareness, and the ability to critically evaluate code they did not write. These are not trivial skills. They take time to develop, and your team needs structured support to build them.

What does investment in teaching architectural thinking actually look like?

> he engineers who will thrive in this environment are the ones who have room to figure out how AI fits into their workflow without being penalized for the learning curve.

Ok, now he's starting to speak to truth.

> System stability, code quality, decision quality, customer outcomes, and team health are better indicators of whether your engineering organization is actually producing value or just producing volume.

What do these look like, as metrics?

> Understanding architecture, debugging complex systems, reasoning about performance and security: these skills are not becoming less important.

What if AI enables growth towards these skills?

> Second, learn to set boundaries with the acceleration trap. Just because you can produce more does not mean you should. Sustainable pace matters.

Pomodoro FTW

> The ones who learn to work with AI deliberately, choosing when to use it and when to think independently, are the ones who will still be thriving in this profession a decade from now.

Planning with AI. Learning what a good plan looks like.

> talk about what you are experiencing.

With the people who matter

> The engineers who are struggling right now are not struggling because they are bad at their jobs. They are struggling because their jobs changed underneath them while the industry celebrated the part that got easier and ignored the parts that got harder.

Why are they struggling though? Not to be reductive, and I have been paying attention to the article. But if it's a matter of not being comfortable with the tools....then practice using the tools.

Perhaps I am more comfortable with experimentation. Perhaps I'm not adopting as much as others. Perhaps my situation is unique.

But I agree: conversation about it is important.

> The industry needs to name this paradox honestly. AI is an incredible tool. It is also placing enormous new demands on the people using it. Both things can be true. Both things need to be addressed.

I like the tone of this article. It's preaching agency (heh) on behalf of the software engineers.

We are assuming more. We are doing more.

We deserve more compensation.

> tools do not build products. People do. And people have limits that no amount of AI can automate away.

Amen.
