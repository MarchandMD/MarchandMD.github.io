# Portfolio for Michael Marchand
A couple of years ago I took an interest in programming. It was October 2017 and I had no idea what I was getting into. 

Fast forward to December 2019, and I'm a little surprised that I've stuck with it. There's been serious frustrations, errors that I had no idea how to handle. And then, seemingly out of nowhere....calm. 

Errors began to frustrate me less; In fact, I grew to welcome them instead of fear them. And that was a turning point in my development: learning how to pay attention to what I was doing, and the feedback I receive. 

Programming has taught me more than I ever expected, and it's now become a part of my life. I have almost a daily practice that is easy for me to maintain. 

And it's become so much more than a way for me to "change my life" or to "prove what i'm worth". Each and every day I find that the thing I've gained from working with Ruby is the thing I never knew I needed: a hobby.

## Projects I'm sharing: 

### Hangman

I don't even know how to describe this; except it's the first program I wrote from complete scratch. I wasn't going to look at anyone else's implementation, I wasn't going to borrower anyone else's code....I wanted to be able to see if I could plan and build something without anyone else's guidance.   

Of course I'd spent a couple of months building the Mastermind and TicTacToe games following tutorials and reviewing someone else's code...but by spending several months slogging through first the tutorial for TicTacToe, and then the task of writing a complete test suite for an existing Mastermind game...I was more than prepared to write a program from scratch.  

I just didn't know it before I started writing.  

And then...in about a weekend, the entire thing came together! I was amazed at how quickly I built it. Now it wasn't the entire thing...but I got so much done in just a couple days. And that was sort of the 'rain-making' moment for me... all the work I have been doing is beginning to coalesce into a real project and skills I can point to with confidence and say: "I did this"

### Mastermind

I didn't want to try and build this game...because when I started the project I felt way out of my league. I had no idea how to build a game from scratch. And making classes and objects work together? that seems pretty far off....

But what I did want to do was develop my comfort with RSpec. 

And so that's what I did. 

I found someone's implementation of the Mastermind game that I liked and wanted to emulate...and I built a Gem complete with an RSpec testing suite, by essentially reverse engineering the program.  

I looked at the code and I did my best to make heads and tales of the entire code...writing tests that may or may not have been necessary, but definitely were functional.  

So the finished project is essentially the same as the other persons project. But my work can be seen in RSpec and the test suite that I built. 

Did I add anything to the game? Not really. But I did learn a hell of a lot about RSpec and testing.
